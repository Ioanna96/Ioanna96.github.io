Introduction

Nostos (from the ancient Greek word νέομαι/neomai, which means “to go or to come back”) refers to the return to one’s home. The concept of nostos was prevalent and introduced for the first time in Homer’s epic poem “Odyssey”, which remains one of the most important works in the Greek literary tradition. We decided to investigate the manifestations of this concept in Greek and Italian works of art, considering our own backgrounds and wishing to draw one more link between two cultures already strongly related.

The E/R Model

We decided that the concept of nostos occupied the central position in our idea, so we connected the related items and entities to it. The first diagram is the model that represents our idea in more specific terms. We decided to also create a more abstract version of it (see second diagram), in order to demonstrate that our general model can be applied in different contexts, as well.

The Metadata Alignment

In this part, we will map the ways in which the same questions are answered by the different standards and ontologies. Working on the metadata alignment allowed us to achieve a deeper understanding of the world of LOD. By examining how the various standards and ontologies describe aspects of an item we saw in practice which models are focused on libraries, archives or museums and we realized which are their strengths and weaknesses.

The Theoretical Model

In this part of the project, we are going to discuss briefly the questions we are going to address, in order to describe our items and clarify which of their aspects we are most interested in.The model is divided in four parts; WHO, which regards the people related to the items; WHAT, which refers to relevant concepts; WHEN, which concerns dates; and WHERE, which includes places.

WHO

We are taking into consideration the people who are involved somehow in our core concept of νοστος, either as intellectuals whose work is centered around this concept or as subjects of works of art related to the concept. We are also interested in the people who made the items related to nostos available to the public in one way or another, mainly publishers and curators. Some of the creators are also subjects, thus we need to distinguish between the entity which creates from the entity which is represented. We also thought it would be useful to differentiate the people from the concepts functioning as subjects/topics. To address these points, we decided to use CIDOC-CRM and Dublin Core. Dublin Core terms were employed to show the different roles an agent can have (creator, subject, contributor) since CIDOC-CRM was not as specific in this aspect (in the role of, is about). It was adequate, though, to define the various roles, major events and references in works of art.

WHAT

This part of our theoretical model is essential. Our idea is centered around the core concept of nostos, so we need to properly address the question “What is the content of our objects?” Since we explore our idea in its manifestations in (Italian and Greek) works of art, we are also interested in the title and the type of representation of each object. With these aims in mind, we decided to use once more CIDOC-CRM to identify the topic and subtopics of our items. Dublin Core terms were also useful for this purpose, since they allowed us to specify even further the subtopics by classifying them as spatial, when a location was an important part of the content, or temporal, when a time period was a significant subtopic. Regarding representation, we used rdf:type, which could be reused for all of our entities, as we will demonstrate later. Finally, we chose rdfs:label to identify the title of the items, since the name provided was not always official, standard or provided by the creator. Therefore, we considered “label” to be a more appropriate predicate than “title”.

WHERE

The aspect of location is secondary in our case, but still highly relevant to our idea. The most important question to address here is “Where was the item created?” since we are examining works of art in the Italian and Greek tradition. Moreover, due to the fact that we are dealing with works of art, it is worthwhile to mention the institution in which they are kept. For these purposes, we used Schema to specify the location where the items were created and CIDOC-CRM to state where the items are being held. When further describing each location, we used Geonames to provide the controlled name and the code of the countries.

WHEN

The aspect of time is the least relevant to our idea. In any case, it might be interesting to examine the period in which the works of art were created. Therefore, for our purposes, we consider the year and/or the time period of creation, when provided by the various institutions. We decided to use Schema to provide this information.

The Conceptual model

The conceptual model is the application of our ideas on how to describe our items, as anticipated in the part of the theoretical model, by using, namely, CIDOC-CRM, DC terms, Geonames and Schema.

URI and Turtle

In this final part, we created two URIs for two central entities in our model: Odysseus and nostos. We decided to use Turtle to encode them, as it is a simple and quite popular language of the Semantic Web. Since we had been introduced to XML in class, we thought it might be a good idea to also get familiarized with Turtle.

The Odyssey

The work of Homer is fundamental in the investigation of the concept of nostos. Homer was the first one to describe with one word the idea of the journey back home, during which the hero has to overcome various obstacles. The epic poem is among the most important works to have survived antiquity and remains an important part of the Greek cultural heritage. We decided to use a random edition of Homer’s Odyssey located in the British Library.

The institution described the entry according to MARC 21 (Machine-readable Cataloging) standard, which is analyzed below:

  • FMT: BK = Format: book
  • Ldr= The leader element, the first digits, which are absent here, indicate the length;
    c,a,m=corrected/revised, language material, monograph a= archival
    22= The two digits are always the same and they signify the indicator count and the subfield code count, respectively
    >00169= This set of digits indicates the base address of data calculated by the computer
    3= the digit in this position shows the encoding level, which in this case is abbreviated level
    u= this part of the leader element concerns the descriptive cataloguing code, which in this case is unknown
    4500=remains always the same
  • 001= this is control number, which is system-generated
  • 008= Fixed Length Data Elements; in this case they only include the date of publication and the language
  • 020= International Standard Book Number (ISBN)
    0= the digit in this position signifies the language, which in this case is English
    19251=the next 5 numbers are the identifier of the publisher
    019= title identifier
    3= the last digit represents the control number
  • 040= the cataloguing source; a refers to an original cataloguing agency and c to a transcribing agency
  • 1000= personal name
  • 245= title information; 00 =no title added entry; a= title
  • 260= publication, distribution
    b= name of publisher, in this case OUP (Oxford University Press)
    c= date of publication, in this case 1980
  • 3= physical description
  • 336= content type; a= content type term (text); 2= source (rdacontent)
  • 337= media type; a= media type term (unmediated); 2= source (rdamedia)
  • 338= carrier type; a= carrier type term (volume); 2= source (rdacarrier)
  • 852= location; 4= shelving control number; 1= Dewey Decimal Classification; a= location; b= sublocation; j= shelving control number

Ithaca

The poem “Ithaca” by Kavafy is a well-known poem that tackles the concept of nostos by making allusions to Homer’s Odyssey. The manuscript of the poem is held at Kavafy Archive, under the jurisdiction of Onassi’s Foundation in Greece.

The institution does not mention the kind of standard that is being used to describe the manuscript, but upon closer look one can easily distinguish ISAD’s (International Standard Archival Description) areas of description. TEI is not mentioned nor is it used.

  • Identity statement area:
    1.1 Reference code= a unique identifier that records the country code, the repository code and a local reference code
    1.2 Title= formal title of item
    1.3 Dates= dates involved in the record of the item, which are not available in this case
    1.4 Level of description: to identify the level of arrangement of this item, which in this case is given as “item”
  • Context area:
    2.1 Name of creator(s)= to identify the creator of the item (in this case C.P. Cavafy)
  • Content and structure area
    3.1 Scope and content= to provide a general idea of the scope (e.g. time periods) and the content (e.g. subject matter) to the end user. The institution here has described the condition of the manuscript.
  • Conditions of access:
    4.3 Language/Scripts of the material= to define the language(s) involved in the object (in this case, “mainly Greek”)
    4.4 Physical characteristics and technical requirements= to provide information on the physical and technical aspects of the item. Details about the handwriting are given in this case.
  • Allied materials:
    5.3 Related units of description: to identify related items

The archive has also listed Access Points, a feature provided by ISAAR(CPF) (International Standard Archival Authority Record for Corporate Bodies, Persons, Families) that allows the description of archival authority records. The institution has listed as subjects Poseidon, poem and notes, but has not differentiated between name and subject access points.

Portrait of Homer

The bust depicts the face of Homer, the author of Odyssey and it is held at the British Museum.

The description of the item does not include references to a specific standard. Therefore, once more, we compared the item’s description with several standards and concluded that the institution uses many elements from CDWA (Categories for the Description of Works of Art).

  • Object type (1.2.)= to specify the particular kind of work of art (in this case: portrait bust)
  • Physical description (13.)= to describe the physical appearance of the item. The institution has provided details about the subject, the materials used and the inscriptions on the object.
  • Culture/Period (4.4.)= to state the name of the culture, nationality or people involved in the creation of the item (Roman, Hellenistic)
  • Date (4.2.)= to identify the date of the creation of the item (2ndC)
  • Findspot (17.3.1.)= to state the excavation location, where the item was found (Baiae)
  • Materials (7.1.)= to describe the materials used for the creation of the object (marble)
  • Dimensions (6.1)= to provide information about the dimensions, size or scale of the work (Height: 57.15 centimetres)
  • Inscription: Inscription Type (8.2) and Inscription Language (8.5)= to state the type (inscription) and language (Greek) of the inscription on the object respectively
  • Curator’s comments: they mostly include elements from CDWA’s area 23, which concerns the ownership history of the item
  • Location, Department (21.1)= to provide information about the item’s current location (Greek & Roman Antiquities Department, On display: G22)
  • Exhibition history (24.)= to describe the history of the item’s public displays

Terracotta calyx-krater

The bowl depicts a scene from Odyssey, namely the encounter of Odysseus with Circe. The enchantress Circe is one of the numerous obstacles Odysseus has to overcome, in order to return to Ithaca. The item is held at the Metropolitan Museum of Art. The institution does not state whether a specific standard was being followed when describing the item, thus we had, once more, to compare the description with various standards.

We could discern that most of the elements could be originating from CDWA. Since these categories were also used before, we will only mention them here:
Period (4.4.), Date (4.2.), Culture (4.4.), Dimensions (6.1), Classification (Object type 1.2.), Credit Line (Ownership history 23.), Location/Department (21.1), Materials (7.1.). In this case, there is also the mention of the possible creator, which is covered by the category 4.1 Creator Descriptions. CDWA accounts for uncertainty in the identification of the creator, which is very useful here: the item is “attributed to the Persephone Painter”.

Hermes Rescues Odysseus from Circe

The painting depicts another scene from the Odyssey: the intervention of Hermes, who helped Odysseus stay immune to the enchantments of Circe. The entry of the item was found at the online museum The Ringling and there is no mention of a standard formulating the description.

In this case as well CDWA is considered the most fitting possibility. The same elements as before are being employed: Date (4.2.), Materials (7.1.), Dimensions (6.1), Credit Line (Ownership history 23.), Location/Department (21.1), Artist ( 4.1 Creator).

Sonetti

Foscolo's collection of poems, and especially the poem Zacinto, is related to our core concept. In his Zacinto Foscolo mentions Odyssey and speaks of exile. The item could be found only as part of editions containing other works of the author. We chose an edition within the Opac catalogue.

The library object is described with ISBD and Marc 21. It is worth-mentioning that unimarc is employed for the authority control of the name of the author.

The ISBD description deals with 5 of the 9 areas provided by the standard:

  • Area 1: Title and statement of responsibility
  • Area 4: Publication, production and distribution
  • Area 5: Material description of the item
  • Area 6: Series area
  • Area 8: Resource identifier

Some of the information that is missing can be found in the labels present under the ISBD cataloging, regarding the type of edition and document and the language. Furthermore, there is an additional resource identifier that follows the ICCU standard.

The Marc 21 description provides similar information with the addition of the authority name record for the author:

  • LEADER → Leader element,it states the record length,the type of record and the bibliographic level.
  • 001 → control number ( Italy/Istituto centrale per il catalogo unico/control number)
  • 003 ItRI → identifier (connected with 001)
  • 005 → date and time of latest transaction
  • 008 → coded information about the record (language and date of publication)
  • 024 → other standard identifier
  • 040 $bita → cataloguing source / $b - Language of cataloging
  • 041 → language code (italian)
  • 100 1 $aFoscolo, Ugo,$d (1778-1827)$0IT\ICCU\CFIV\001434$4aut → Main entry personal name; $a - Personal name and surname; $d - Dates associated with a name; $0 - Authority record control number or standard number
  • 245. → title statement (in this case there is also the statement of responsibility)
  • 260 → publication and distribution/date
  • 300 → physical description (in this case just pages and dimensions of the book)
  • 500 → general note field
  • 790 → local added entry personal name/forename

    • Finally, it is possible to access the authority record (with unimarc) for Foscolo with information about the author, in particular about variants of his name and his profession with the source where this information was taken from. The last two elements of the description provide the identification of the resource within SBN and ISNI.

Enea e Anchise fuggono da Troia in fiamme

The painting represents a scene from Eneide, more specifically the escape of Enea from Troy. After the Greeks destroyed Troy, Enea fled the city with his father on his back. The item is a ceiling painting held within the project Culturaitalia and physically kept at Palazzo Pitti in Florence.

The item has been catalogued with METS(metadata encoding and transmission standard). This standard is often used alongside other standards. In this case the other standards used were VRA Core, for the description of the image, MIX, for the technical elements required to manage digital image collections and PREMIS, the standard used to support the preservation of digital objects.

This encoding has 5 of the seven sections of a Mets document.

  • METS HEADER: information about the creator and the ipowner.
  • Descriptive Metadata dmdSec: this is the section with the description of the item, entirely encoded with VRA.
    Inside the tag vra:agentSet it is possible to see information about the author and relative role and dates of work, about his collaborators and their working period. The tag vra:descriptionSet> allows the description of the item and of its collection.
    vra:locationSet is the container of the information about the physiscal location of the object.
    vra:materialSet provides the tags for identification of the image displayed.
    Enclosed within vra:rightsSet we can find the holder of the rights of this image and of the number of the item.
    Lastly under the vra:sourceSet There is the description of the topic and the title, the worktype and the relation with the image.
  • Administrative Metadata amdSec: this section contains administrative information about the files of the digital object and it is encoded with MIX and PREMIS.
    First mix:BasicDigitalObjectInformation contains the basic information about the object,the file type and its value.
    Then through mix:BasicImageInformation the design it’s explained in format, weight and height of the picture and type of colour.
    mix:ImageCaptureMetadata holds the information about the scanner used to create the digital images.
    Lastly premis:rights stores the information about the rights and licenses to the image.
  • File Section fileSec: contains the elements that are used to group together related files.
  • Structural map structMap: defines the hierarchical structure that is presented to the user in order to let him navigate through the object. In this case we only have one a basic structure with just one object.

Nostos, il ritorno

This item is a movie, held in the Rosebud Cinema Archive of Reggio Emilia. It follows the adventures of Odysseus during his trip back to Ithaca.

Unfortunately this archive doesn’t follow a clear standard and for this item there are few information, displayed in a unclear way. In fact, all the people that have worked in the project are grouped together under the cast and credits label, with the exception of the director. The comment section is the only one that goes deep in the topic, giving an explanation and a point of view from which to examine the message of the movie. It can be said that the few labels present follow, more or less, the dublin core standard for the notions of title, creator (the director) and description (the plot).

Calco (Virgil’s bust)

The bust represents the portrait of Virgil, the author of Eneid, which is rather central in our idea of connecting the Italian and Greek tradition on the basis of the concept of nostos. Virgil was and is a primary figure in the area of Italian letters. His bust can be found in Catalogo Generale dei beni culturali.

The description goes in depth and follows the ICCD standard of cataloging, that can be accessed through the ‘Scheda completa’ in pdf format.

The first area CD exhibits the general information about the amministrative level of the codification of the object. Inside we can find:

  • OA: refers to art works and objects
  • NCT - CODICE UNIVOCO NCTR : code with specifications for the region and the number inside the general catalogue
  • ESC contains the notions of Ente schedatore and Ente competente, and specification about the coding organization.
  • LC - LOCALIZZAZIONE: refers to the place where the object is held starting from the province and municipal and specifications about the building where the object is held and the type of cultural organization that holds it.
  • OG – OGGETTO: generic information about the physical object
  • DT – CRONOLOGIA: period of creation
  • AU - DEFINIZIONE CULTURALE: only section referred to cultural aspect of the object
  • MT- DATI TECNICI: section about the physical qualities of the object, more specific width, length and depth.
  • CO – CONSERVAZIONE: section about the condition of the object
  • DA - DATI ANALITICI: information about the subject and about the inscription on the object
  • TU - CONDIZIONE GIURIDICA E VINCOLI: section about the juridical condition ot the item.
  • CM – COMPILAZIONE: information about the registration regarding both the date and the person who is responsible for its encoding
  • AN – ANNOTAZIONI: final observation and annotation regarding the object.

Eneide

Eneide is the famous epic poem written by Virgil and remains to our day one of the most important works of the Italian culture. It follows the adventures of Enea, after the loss of his home. In one sense, nostos is impossible for him and we thought this was an importan aspect to be taken into consideration; the other side of the coin. Since returning home is not an option for him after Troy was destroyed, his search for a new place to call home is an interesting and different take on nostos. In this case, nostos acquires a new dimension: the trip towards a home that has not been found yet. We decided to include a manuscript of the story. This item is one of the oldest representation we have left of this poem. It is held in the Vatican library and it is catalogued under the name Vaticano latino 3867.

This institution states of cataloguing its items through the Marcand ISBD standard, but for this item it was only possible to examine a catalographic description, online or in a pdf format, that, doesn’t follow clearly those standard. Anyway, many of the labels can be aligned with the Marc standard as can be seen in the metadata alignment section of the project.

In particular the labels can be aligned in this way:
The title (ocelli nominum) can be considered as Marc 245;
The language label can be referred as part of Marc 008;
The description section about topic and content and the type of media can fall under Marc 650, 336 and 337;
The information presented about the creation period and place can be aligned with Marc 008 and 260 that refer to the publishing date and place;
Finally the place where the item is held nowadays can be stated with Marc 852.
